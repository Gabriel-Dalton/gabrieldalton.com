Sustainable Technology Week comes to Vancouver.

In 2025 we worked with Mayor Ken Sim and the City of Vancouver to officially proclaim Sustainable Technology Week — the first time the environmental cost of the web has made it onto a major Canadian city's civic calendar.

Most cities have a sustainability strategy. Almost none of them name technology in it. The energy a city spends running its websites, running its data centres, running the hundreds of small SaaS tools quietly pulling power in the background — most of that footprint never gets counted. We wanted to change that, in our city, on the calendar.

What we proclaimed.

Sustainable Technology Week is a civic acknowledgement that the web has a footprint — and that the footprint is something a city can choose to take seriously. The proclamation commits to public conversations about energy-efficient design, accessibility, and the responsible deployment of AI. It's a small thing on paper. It's a real thing in policy: it gives staff something to point to.

How it came together.

The Mayor's office was generous and curious — the conversation took a few months. We worked from a simple thesis: every council in Canada will, eventually, need a position on the environmental cost of digital infrastructure. Vancouver could go first.

The web has a footprint. The footprint is something a city can choose to take seriously.

What's next.

The work in 2026 is about turning a proclamation into procurement guidance — the small, unglamorous decisions about which vendors a city signs with, which platforms it deprecates, and how it measures the carbon and water cost of its own digital operations. We're also bringing in VCASSE on the AI side: most cities will buy more AI in the next three years than in the previous thirty. Someone needs to be in the room asking what it costs.

If you work in a city, a province, a foundation, or an enterprise that procures digital infrastructure at scale and you want to compare notes — write me.
