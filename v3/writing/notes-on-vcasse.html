Notes on building VCASSE.

Why Vancouver needs a centre for AI safety, sustainability, and ethics, and what the first six months of building one have actually looked like.

VCASSE, the Vancouver Centre for AI Safety, Sustainability & Ethics, is a subsidiary of Oasis of Change, Inc. It's small, deliberate, and very much under construction. This is a working note, not a press release.

Why this, why here.

The conversation about AI safety in Canada is mostly happening in Toronto and Montreal, for excellent reasons. But the cost of leaving the West Coast out of that conversation is real. British Columbia is going to deploy AI in healthcare, in education, in resource management, in classrooms full of teenagers, faster than the policy infrastructure can keep up. We need a place that keeps showing up to those decisions.

Vancouver also has something most AI safety hubs don't: an unusually strong sustainability and Indigenous policy ecosystem to think alongside. AI safety, when it's any good, isn't only a question of "will the model do the right thing." It's a question of "what land, what water, what people are paying for this thing." That's a conversation Vancouver is good at.

What we're working on.

  • A municipal AI procurement playbook. Practical guidance for how a city government should buy, deploy, and decommission AI systems.
  • A youth advisory practice. Most AI policy is written without consulting the people who'll live with it longest. We're building a small, structured way for young Canadians to weigh in.
  • A sustainability framework for AI workloads. Plain-language guidance for measuring the carbon, water, and attention costs of common AI deployments, written for nontechnical decision-makers.

What I'm learning.

Three things, six months in:

  1. Smaller is faster. The temptation to be a research institute is enormous. We resist it. The faster you can get a deliverable in front of an actual policymaker, the faster the next door opens.
  2. Sustainability and safety are the same conversation. When you take the cost question seriously, "is this AI safe" and "is this AI worth running" become the same sentence.
  3. Convening matters more than publishing. The most useful thing we've done so far is a small private dinner with the right twelve people. Three policy memos came out of it. None of them have my name on them. Good.

How to get involved.

VCASSE is small on purpose. If you work in BC government, in K-12 or post-secondary, in Indigenous policy, or at any organization deploying AI in Canada and you want to compare notes, write me. We'll have a public site soon enough; in the meantime, the door is open.
