Digital Sustainability 14 March 2026 6 min read

Digital sustainability is the missing piece of tech literacy.

If we want to teach people what technology is doing to the world, we have to teach them what it costs — in carbon, in water, in attention, in trust.

Tech literacy, as taught in most schools and most companies, is mostly a literacy of capability: how to use a tool, how to evaluate a source, how to spot a phishing email. These are useful. They are not enough.

What's missing is a literacy of cost. The energy used to train a model. The water poured through cooling towers to keep a data centre online. The attention pulled out of a human being by an interface designed to be unleavable. The trust handed over, click by click, to systems we can't read.

Four costs nobody puts in the syllabus.

Carbon. A modern web page weighs roughly 2.6 MB. A modest organisation's website, served to a few hundred thousand people a year, can quietly emit as much CO₂ as a transatlantic flight. Most teams have never looked. Most teams could halve it without anyone noticing the difference — except the planet.

Water. A single hyperscale data centre can consume millions of litres a day. The communities living next door usually find out when their wells run dry. We don't usually call that a software cost. It is.

Attention. Every notification is a small act of theft. Every infinite scroll is a small act of theft. By the time a teenager finishes high school, they have spent more hours staring into a feed than reading every book they were assigned. Tech literacy that ignores this is comfort, not literacy.

Trust. Generative AI is busy filling the public record with statements nobody is willing to be accountable for. The cost of that is borne by everyone who tries to make a decision later.

What a curriculum could look like.

If I were rewriting a tech-literacy unit tomorrow, I'd ask three questions of every tool we taught:

  1. What does it cost to run, and who pays?
  2. Who is excluded from using it, and what do we do about that?
  3. What does it ask of you that you didn't agree to?

None of these questions is technical. All of them are answerable. They turn an inert "how does this app work" into a real conversation about who builds tools, who profits from them, and who lives with the bill.

Tech literacy without cost literacy is comfort. It is not literacy.

The version I want.

The version of digital sustainability I keep arguing for isn't a feel-good environmental sticker on the side of a product. It's a way of seeing — a refusal to pretend that the web is a free service and the data centre is somebody else's problem. Once you've started seeing the costs, you can't really stop. That's the point.

If you teach, build, fund, or set policy and you want to compare notes on what this looks like in practice — write me.
